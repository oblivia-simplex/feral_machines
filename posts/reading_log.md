# A Scanner Darkly, by Philip K. Dick

![cover](../img/reading_log/a_scanner_darkly.jpg)

The first time I'd read this book must've sometime near the end of my
teens. I remember there being a kind of forbidden allure to the
underworld that Dick depicts here, at the time—a world that, even when
it's laid bare in all its abject dysfunction, retains a sense of the
tragic, even a sense of the sublime. It was a world I was just beginning
to explore, myself. The same friend who gave me the book was one of my
first gateways into that world.

This time around, I listened to *A Scanner Darkly* in audiobook form,
read by Paul Giamatti (formally, the book's told in the third person,
but all the same I was left with the impression that Giamatti would've
made a far more compelling Bob Arctor than Keanu Reeves does—as natural
as Reeves is at being an empty vessel). I'd bought the book on my phone,
on the first stop on a long flight home. What struck me this time around
was how boring, bleak, and tedious the addict's life was. Seeing people
who I'd respected, even loved, lose themselves in it, so many times
between the first read and the second, put a finer point on it.

# God Jr, by Dennis Cooper

![cover](../img/reading_log/god_jr.jpg)

I read two books this year that captured something or other that's
deeply true of parenthood, without sentimentalism or cynicism. The first
was *God Jr.*, the second was Roth's *American Pastoral*. Both dealt
with the agony of being absolutely beholden to someone who remains, when
all the chips are down, unknowable.
