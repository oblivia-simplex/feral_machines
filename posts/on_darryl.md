

Where do I even start? I don't think I've ever read anything funnier. The books I've read that had me howling and cackling like this, I could probably count them on one hand. It's a hard thing to do with the novel. But Jackie Ess knocks it out of the park, a hundred home runs in a row. 

_Darryl_ is a book about cuckoldry, but what's cuckoldry about for Darryl? "We cuckolds", he tells his friend,

> are the only sexual orientation that's about the truth. Everybody else is about performance, pleasure, recognition. Is that why people seem to hate us so much lately? Maybe watching and listening are the bravest things a guy can do. Can you face your own inferiority? Can you watch yourself be replaced?

In his unpresumptive and innocent way, Darryl is a mystic. Jackie Ess gives him all the room in the world to make a holy fool of himself, but she never sneers at him, never hides behind cynicism. He's always a little ridiculous, but not _simply_ so. From start to finish, there's never any doubt that what Darryl's doing _matters_, that something real's at stake.

> I went for a walk down by the Willamette and thought a lot about what connects my life to life, the old protein machine from soup to present. Maybe what I like so much about being in nature sometimes is seeing this level where it's all automatic. Does that make any sense? It's confirming and it's relaxing. There's a feeling of being left behind by all of it. By people too. It's intense, humiliating, and kind of numb. I think I must not be wired right. Well, I know that. 
> 
> But Bill and Mindy don't really need me, right? That's the joke. But the other puzzle is this. Why can't I seem to move past this moment where everything is revealed, confirmed, the precise moment when we break the connection? The door, which was open a crack, closing. The rotary dial phone, clattering to the floor. My wife, lost to the brute. And I'm lost too, in this kind of ecstasy of shame that takes me out of myself, and deeper into this kind of ocean roar feeling. 

We'll hear echoes of this a passage throughout the book, right down to its very last line. And this is what he's grappling with. Will he ever step through the door? Will he find a way to connect with life and truth that isn't vicarious? Will he get any closer without falling to pieces and plummeting?

_Darryl_ is a comedy, but it's not frivolous. Darryl's goofy humility and naivete disarm us, and so we let ourselves be led up to the volcano's edge, defenseless.

Darryl is a character who will be living in your head for years. He'll be there, sitting, quietly, in the corner, waving shyly, now and then. He's one of the most tenderly and brutally realized characters, maybe not in literary history, but certainly in the little swath of that history I've walked through. The other characters in the books are ciphers, mostly, but memorable ones. You certainly see as much of them as Darryl, himself, does, and they leave an impression. Clive, Bill, Mindy, Kit, Oothoon, Satori, Moonbeam, each of them feels so intensely vivid when I say their names. Somebody once said something to me about Dickens' characters, and I think it applies here: they might be flat, but they're vibrating extremely fast, they're _so much_ of what they are. The same goes for _Darryl's_ supporting cast. 

This might not mean much to anyone who doesn't know her, and I'm pretty sure that everyone who does is probably already gearing up to read this book, if they haven't already, but _there's so much of Jackie in this book!_ This book's a delight for a lot of the same reasons that Jackie's good company. The same sense of humour that comes across in conversation and in her posts, the same searching seriousness, the same brilliance, and the same bewildering talent that she brings to everything she touches.

So, go and read this book. It's going to absolutely delight you. You're going to sit down and tear through it in two or three days, tops, because you'll have to fight yourself to put it down. It's a book that'll remind you what novels can do. You'll be replaying scenes in your head, weeks after you've put the book down. And when you give in and read it a second time, it'll be even more striking than you remembered.
